**Ali Mountain**
**C:** The train ride up Ali Mountain is a must-do. It's one of Taiwan's oldest trains.

**Sun Moon Lake**
**C:** Sun Moon Lake is the largest lake in Taiwan.
**G:** Then they must have boating excursions.

**Shui-Li Snake Kiln**
**C:** I recommend you visit Shui-Li Snake Kiln in Nantou. You can even take classes and make your own pottery.
**G:** Where can we pick up some ceramics while we're in Taiwan?

**Tainan / Anping Fort**
**G:** I've heard that Tainan is one of Taiwan's most historic places.
**C:** Yes, that's correct. There's even a fort that was built by the Dutch in the 17th century.

**Queen's Head Rock**
**G:** The Queen's Head Rock? What's that?
**C:** It's a rock formation that looks just like a woman's head.

### Scene I: Checking Out a Night Market (pp. 110–111)

**Track 52**

**Setting:** After checking in, a guest approaches the concierge.
**Characters:** **C** = Concierge | **G** = Guest

---

**Dialogue:**

**C:** Good afternoon, sir. What can I do for you?

**G:** I'm flying to Hong Kong tomorrow. Before I go, I'd like to check out a local night market. Do you have any suggestions?

**C:** Certainly. Why not go to the nearby Liuho Night Market? There are hundreds of food **stands** there.

**G:** How long does it take to get there?

**C:** It takes about 20 minutes by taxi.

**G:** What local **specialties** would you suggest I try?

**C:** You should try the oyster omelet and the aiyu gelatin.

**G:** Oyster omelets and aiyu gelatin? What are they?

**C:** Oyster omelets are **mainly** made from eggs and oysters. They're **topped** with a sweet and **spicy** sauce. As for the aiyu gelatin, it's usually served with lemon juice and honey.

**G:** What else would you recommend?

**C:** If you like noodles, try some duck noodles. The duck has a unique **flavor** when it's **mixed** with the noodle soup.

**G:** Thanks for the advice. It sounds like it's going to be a **culinary** adventure!

**C:** You're welcome, sir. I hope you have a great time at Liuho Night Market.

### Scene II: At the Lantern Festival (pp. 112–113)

**Track 53**

**Setting:** A guest wants to know about the Kaohsiung Lantern Festival.
**Characters:** **G** = Guest | **C** = Concierge

---

**Dialogue:**

**G:** I've heard the Kaohsiung Lantern Festival is a lot of fun. Could you tell me more about it?

**C:** Yes, sir. It's **held** by the Love River, as you might've already heard. There will be lots of lanterns on **display**.

**G:** If I do decide to go, what else can I expect to see?

**C:** There will be a laser **fountain** and a water and fire dance show. The **folk art** **performances** are really good too. And don't forget the **fireworks**.

**G:** Did you go to the festival last year?

**C:** Yes, I did. In fact, I **take part** almost every year. And this year, I'm **volunteering** at the event.

**G:** Is that so?

**C:** Yes, I will help people who don't speak Chinese.

**G:** Well, that's very nice of you. Is there anything else I should **keep in mind**?

**C:** Well, if you go with a group, I'd suggest you bring a cell phone in case you get lost. The place is very big.

**G:** Thank you for providing me with so much information.

**C:** It's my pleasure. Maybe I'll see you there.

#### Language Center — Scene II (p. 113)

**A — Sentence Patterns**

**Pattern:** S. + V. . . . in case S. + V. . . .

- I'm taking a map in case I get lost.
- You should take an umbrella with you in case it rains.
- I brought some snacks in case you get hungry.
- We'd better take a little extra money, just in case we need it.
- In case there is an emergency, please call this number.
- In case we saw something special, we always took a camera.

> *Note: There is no comma before* in case*, but a comma is needed to separate* just in case *from the rest of a sentence.*

---

**B — Word Usage: Usage of "do" as an Auxiliary**

**To avoid repetition of a verb:**
- I love traveling as much as you do.
- A: I exercise every day. B: I do too. (So do I.)

**To emphasize the main verb:**
- You do look younger in that suit.
- Do be careful. It's very dark outside.

### Scene III: Fun in Kenting (pp. 114–115)

**Track 54**

**Setting:** The concierge helps a guest with his travel plans.
**Characters:** **C** = Concierge | **G** = Guest

---

**Dialogue:**

**C:** How are you today, sir? Can I help you with something?

**G:** Well, I'm **heading** south to Kenting after I check out. Do you know what the weather's usually like there?

**C:** It's sunny almost all year round. The skies are always clear, and it seldom rains.

**G:** Great! I've heard that Kenting is **famous for** its beaches, and it's a **heaven** for water sports lovers.

**C:** It sure is. Kenting is also one of the best locations for **surfing** in Taiwan. And if you're a music person, you'll want to check out Spring Scream.

**G:** Actually, it's the music festival that **interests** me most about the place. I've heard it's held **annually**, in early April.

**C:** Right. Both local and **overseas** rock bands will perform there. And tens of thousands of music **fans** will **jam** the streets of Kenting at that time.

**G:** Sounds exciting! By the way, do you have any idea how much a ticket for Spring Scream is?

**C:** A five-day **pass** costs NT$2,000. I could book the ticket for you if you like.

**G:** That would be great. No wonder my friends all say they'll come again in the future. It sounds **awesome**! I can't wait!

#### Language Center — Scene III (p. 115)

**A — Sentence Patterns**

**Pattern 1:** (It's / There's) no / small / little wonder (that) S. + V. . . .

- No wonder most of my friends said this is a good restaurant.
- Small wonder that the hotel runs a good business.
- It's no wonder the sink is often clogged.
- It's no wonder many guests choose to check into that hotel.
- With such nice beaches, there's little wonder that Kenting is so popular with tourists.

**Pattern 2:** It is sth / sb that S. + V. . . . (Emphasize Structure)

*Example:* The valet parked Mr. Chen's car in the garage.

- It was the **valet** that / who parked Mr. Chen's car in the garage. *(emphasizes the valet)*
- It was **Mr. Chen's car** that the valet parked in the garage. *(emphasizes Mr. Chen's car)*
- It was **in the garage** that the valet parked Mr. Chen's car. *(emphasizes in the garage)*

> *Note: If the emphasized part is human, we can use* who *instead of* that.
